## On the Arc from Code to Architecture — and Back to Building

I started my career writing code. Then something more interesting happened: I became the person who could speak both languages.

Over nearly two decades I moved through techno-functional, solution architecture, and enterprise architecture roles — sitting in the space between the business and the developers, often managing the engineering teams while owning the design decisions above them. That in-between role is where strategy actually gets made. Not in the boardroom, and not in the IDE — but in the translation layer between them.

I don't call myself a coder anymore. That's an accurate description, not a limitation. What I am is someone who understands what's happening in the codebase well enough to make better architectural decisions, ask better questions, and call it when an engineering estimate doesn't pass the smell test.

What AI changed is the prototyping calculus. When I can spin up a working agentic workflow in an afternoon using Claude and Replit, the old boundary between "I architect it" and "someone else builds it" gets interestingly blurry. So I lean into that. I build to think, not to ship.

That's what you'll find here — the work of a technical strategist who never fully left the building, and has found a new reason to get closer to it again.

## How I Learn: Riding Waves, Not Chasing Trends

There's a pattern in how I've built expertise over nearly two decades that I only recognized in hindsight. Every few years, a new wave hits enterprise technology. Most people wait to see if it's real. I tend to go in early — not recklessly, but deliberately.

**Service Management → Automation → AI.** That's the through-line.

I earned ITIL Expert when service management was the language of enterprise IT transformation. I added DevOps and Scrum certifications when agile was reshaping how technology gets delivered. I got into Automation Anywhere RPA and Celonis process mining when intelligent automation was the headline at every analyst conference — years before "AI" became the default word for all of it.

What I understood then that still holds: the use cases never really changed. Reduce the manual. Eliminate the handoff. Surface the inefficiency. Free humans for higher-judgment work. The capability ceiling is just dramatically higher now.

By the time I earned TOGAF Enterprise Architecture Practitioner in 2024, I'd been doing enterprise architecture for years — the credential formalized what the work had already proved. That's how I prefer to learn: lead with curiosity, follow with rigor.

The AI chapter is the same pattern, just faster. In 2025 alone I went deep on RAG and agentic AI architecture, AI in regulated environments like government and healthcare, and AI product management. In March 2026 I completed Anthropic's AI Fluency Framework — because if I'm building with Claude, I want to understand how it actually thinks, not just what it produces.

**The one chapter that surprises people:** in 2023 I went deep on sustainability — ESG materiality, GRI and TCFD reporting frameworks, circular economy, IBM Envizi. Not because it was required. Because I saw it coming as the next governance and technology intersection that enterprises would need to navigate. AI governance and ESG governance have more in common than most practitioners realize: both are about accountability, auditability, and stakeholder trust at scale.

That's the instinct I try to bring to everything I build and advise on. Not what's trending. What's coming.

## On Being a Woman in Enterprise Tech

I've spent nearly two decades in rooms where I was often the only woman, sometimes the only person asking the uncomfortable question, and occasionally the person who had to fight to be heard before being proven right.

I'm not bitter about it. I'm motivated by it.

The women in tech who've surrounded me throughout my career — mentors, peers, the ones who came up beside me and the ones I've tried to pull up with me — are the reason I believe this industry can keep getting better. I try to reflect that in how I work, what I publish, and who I make space for.
